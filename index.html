Algorithmic Bias: When the Computer Gets It Wrong

11.25%

Lower Accuracy for Africans

Studies show that FRT demonstrates an 11.25% lower True Positive Rate (TPR) for African populations compared to Caucasians. This is not a glitch; this is systemic failure encoded, translating directly into the disproportionate criminalization of communities of color.

Source: Sarridis et al. (2025)

+39.89%

Error Spike for Older Black Women

The biases compound: African females over 60 years old face a +39.89% higher misidentification rate compared to the lowest-error demographic. This highlights the crushing reality that the technology is least reliable for those who already face the highest scrutiny.

Source: Sarridis et al. (2025)

96%

Failure Rate If Used Alone

Former Detroit Police Chief James Craig acknowledged that if the city's officers were to use facial recognition by itself, it would yield misidentifications "96% of the time."

Source: Innocence Project (2023)

The Root of the Failure: Bad Design Meets the Real World

The core problem is twofold: biased model design and inherent structural limitations. Algorithms are historically trained on non-diverse datasets (a "lack of diverse representations").

Source: Sarridis et al.

Furthermore, accuracy plummets in real-world environments where variables like "bad angles and poor lighting make it less accurate." For people with darker skin, poor lighting often results in less visible feature definition for the camera, contributing to higher misidentification rates. No amount of tweaking can fix a system fundamentally flawed by light, shadow, and biased input data.

Source: Johnson and Johnson

Robert Williams (Detroit, 2020) was the first known person in the U.S. to be wrongfully arrested based solely on a faulty facial recognition match. He was detained for 30 hours for a crime he did not commit.

Privacy & Authoritarian Control: The Loss of Anonymous Life

Privacy is the right to move and associate freely without permanent observation. FRT shatters this, enabling a severe "power imbalance between the state and the citizenry." The commercialization of surveillance, where private companies scrape billions of images and sell the infrastructure to police, bypasses democratic oversight entirely.

Source: Markus and Miller

The ultimate outcome of unchecked biometric tracking is the slide into tyranny. The capability is already weaponized: the Chinese Communist Party's mass surveillance of the Uyghur Muslim population relies heavily on facial and gait recognition to track individuals, restrict their freedoms, and enforce ethnic cleansing. Allowing these tools to flourish in a democracy dangerously normalizes the technology of oppression.

Fourth Amendment Violation: The Comprehensive Chronicle

The Fourth Amendment secures "the privacies of life" against arbitrary government power. While a fleeting glance in public is not a search, the Supreme Court ruled in Carpenter v. United States that long-term tracking of location data is unconstitutional without a warrant because it creates a "comprehensive chronicle of the user’s past movements."

Source: Bass, Adriana (2022)

FRT as a Perpetual Search

FRT is far more pervasive than traditional methods. It automatically links every sighting of a person's face across massive networks of cameras to their personal identity. This passive, continuous data collection constitutes the exact kind of unwarranted "search" the Supreme Court ruled against. Every public outing becomes a piece of evidence stored in a permanent, searchable database.

Eroding Probable Cause

If the state can identify you instantly and track your entire movement history, the constitutional requirement for police to establish probable cause before an investigation begins is effectively gutted. Because FRT transforms random public observation into a searchable, limitless record, its deployment is fundamentally incompatible with our constitutional safeguards.

First Amendment Violation: The Chilling Effect

The ability to speak freely and organize without fear is non-negotiable in a free society. Biometric surveillance is a tool of intimidation that actively discourages the exercise of these rights, generating a dangerous "chilling effect."

Silencing Dissent

As found in research by Bass (2022), Black Lives Matter protestors "feared that the police would retaliate against them" since law enforcement could easily identify them using FRT.

Source: Bass, Adriana (2022)

If citizens know they can be permanently logged while attending a protest, organizing a union, or joining a political rally, they "may hesitate to participate," weakening the very fabric of democracy.

The Loss of Anonymity

The threat of permanent identification induces self-censorship. Activism, political speech, and even association with certain groups rely on anonymity to protect participants from harassment or retaliation by the state or private entities. FRT eliminates that layer of protection, turning constitutional freedoms into high-risk behaviors.
